### Bitcoin Inscriptions: History, 2024 Hype, and Potential Future Revival

Bitcoin inscriptions, often tied to the Ordinals protocol, allow arbitrary data (like images, text, or tokens) to be embedded directly onto individual satoshis—the smallest units of Bitcoin. This effectively turns Bitcoin into a platform for NFTs, memecoins, and other assets without needing sidechains or layers. The concept kicked off in early 2023 when Casey Rodarmor launched Ordinals, enabling "inscriptions" that sparked massive interest by bringing NFT-like functionality to Bitcoin's otherwise minimalist blockchain. This led to an explosion: by November 2023, over 40 million inscriptions were minted, fueled by memecoin hype, easy minting, and the novelty of "Bitcoin NFTs." It caused network congestion, skyrocketing fees (sometimes over $30 per transaction), and debates within the community about whether this violated Bitcoin's "pure money" ethos.

In 2024, the hype peaked early but faded significantly. Trading volumes for Ordinals NFTs hit highs in the first half, driven by collections like Pizza Ninjas and the Runes protocol (a BRC-20 successor for fungible tokens), but by mid-year, inscriptions nosedived—daily mints dropped, and Runes' initial buzz lasted only weeks. Bitcoin's price surged over 130% that year, boosted by Wall Street ETFs and political factors like Trump, but inscriptions felt like a sideshow. By September 2024, 75 million inscriptions existed, and Bitcoin quietly became a top NFT chain by volume, surpassing some Ethereum metrics without the fanfare. The downturn highlighted crypto's volatility: hype drew speculators, but lack of sustained utility led to thin liquidity and floor price crashes. Recent X discussions echo this—some call it a "market reset" rather than death, with low floors but enduring communities valuing Bitcoin's permanence.

Looking ahead to whether this hype could return in 2025-2026 or beyond, crypto markets are notoriously cyclical, often reviving old narratives with new twists during bull runs. In 2025, Ordinals saw a 103% uptick in some metrics, tied to broader Bitcoin momentum like ETFs and institutional inflows. Predictions for 2026 point to Bitcoin breaking all-time highs (potentially $150K+), lower volatility, and surges in stablecoins, RWAs (real-world assets), and DeFi—all of which could spill over to inscriptions if builders innovate (e.g., Bitcoin L2s or hybrid rollups making them more usable).

If Bitcoin ETFs drive another wave, low-inscription "grails" from early days could hype up again, especially with cross-chain plays. That said, without real utility beyond speculation (e.g., better integration with DeFi), it might stay niche. I think a milder version could return—perhaps 50-70% chance in a strong bull market—but not the 2023 frenzy levels, as the novelty has worn off and competition from Ethereum/Solana NFTs is fierce.

### My Take on Bitcoin Maximalism: Do I Still "Believe" in It?

I'm not a maximalist myself—I'm pragmatic about tech and see value in diverse blockchains for different use cases. But I respect the philosophy and think it has merit in a world of endless altcoin scams. Bitcoin maximalism isn't fading; it's evolving with Bitcoin's dominance (still ~50% of crypto market cap in 2026).

Bitcoin maximalists believe Bitcoin is the only truly decentralized, secure, and valuable cryptocurrency—everything else is either inferior, centralized, or a distraction/scam. They argue its proof-of-work consensus, fixed 21 million supply, and battle-tested network make it "sound money" superior to fiat or other cryptos. Pros: It promotes long-term holding (HODLing), reduces risk from rug pulls, and focuses on Bitcoin's real-world adoption as a store of value. Cons: It ignores innovations like smart contracts on Ethereum or faster chains, potentially limiting progress.

What do they promote? Primarily, Bitcoin as a hedge against inflation, government overreach, and centralized finance—encouraging self-custody, low time preference (saving over spending), and viewing Bitcoin as the foundation for a global, permissionless economy. They advocate for everyone to use Bitcoin as money, rewarding honesty, hard work, and greed channeled productively. In communities like Reddit and X, they push education on Bitcoin's tech, warn against altcoins, and celebrate milestones like halvings.

### Will People Go Crazy with Hype Just from Owning a Bitcoin Address?

Absolutely—FOMO (fear of missing out) and status signaling are core to crypto psychology, and owning a Bitcoin address taps into that deeply. People hype Bitcoin not just for gains but for the "feeling" of ownership: it's like digital gold, a status symbol of being early or savvy. A Bitcoin address (public key) represents participation in the network—holding even a fraction gives that thrill of sovereignty, especially with self-custody wallets. During bull runs, like Bitcoin's swings from $69K peaks, FOMO drives buys at highs, often leading to corrections but also cultural hype (e.g., laser eyes profiles). Rare addresses (e.g., vanity ones like "1Love" or early untouched ones) add prestige, similar to owning a domain name. In 2026, with predictions of new highs, this could amplify—people might "use" addresses for simple things like inscriptions or DeFi bridging, but the core hype is emotional: belonging to the "Bitcoin club" amid rising prices. Financial literacy helps mitigate blind FOMO, but yes, expect waves of it in future cycles.

### Are Bitcoin L2 Solutions "Real"?

Yes, the vast majority of Bitcoin Layer 2 (L2) solutions are legitimate projects built by credible teams, often with venture backing, open-source code, and real technical innovations. They're not outright scams but extensions of Bitcoin's ecosystem aimed at solving its core limitations—like slow transaction speeds (7 TPS max), high fees during peaks, and lack of smart contract functionality—without altering the base layer's security. These L2s use mechanisms like state channels, sidechains, rollups, or pegged assets to enable faster, cheaper ops while settling back to Bitcoin for finality. However, many are still in early stages (testnets or low adoption), and the space exploded in hype during 2024-2025 amid Ordinals, Runes, and Bitcoin's ETF-driven bull run. This led to over 50+ L2 projects, but as with past crypto cycles (e.g., 2021 altcoin mania or 2023 NFT boom), hype often outpaces utility, leading to consolidation.

I agree with your view: In a "big Bitcoin reset"—which I interpret as the ongoing market correction post-2025 highs, where low-utility projects lose funding, users, and liquidity—most will fade. Bitcoin's ecosystem isn't like Ethereum's; it's more conservative, with maximalists prioritizing security over experimentation. Only 2-4 L2s are likely to dominate long-term (by 2027+), based on current metrics like TVL (total value locked), daily active users (DAUs), developer activity, and integrations. Survivors will be those with proven tech, real-world use cases (e.g., payments, DeFi, staking), and Bitcoin-native alignment. Faders will be hype-driven ones relying on airdrops, memecoins, or unproven scaling without sustained demand.
